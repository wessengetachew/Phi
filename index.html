Section 1 · The Lift Survival Framework

The Recovery Formula — How a Jump Size Encodes a Prime

The Lift Survival Function C(x) is the product over all primes p ≤ x of the fraction of integers in the modular ring {1,…,M} that remain coprime to p. When a new prime p enters the product, C(x) drops by an exact multiplicative factor — and the size of that drop uniquely identifies which prime caused it.

Definition — Lift Survival Function
C(x)  =  ∏p ≤ x (p² − 2) / (p² − 1) C(x) is a step function, constant between primes, dropping at each prime p with jump size s = 1/(p(p−1)).

The Jump Size s

At each prime p, the relative drop in C(x) is the jump size:

Jump Size Definition
s  =  1 / (p(p − 1))  =  1/2, 1/6, 1/20, 1/42, …   for p = 2, 3, 5, 7, … s → 0 as p → ∞. The largest possible jump is s = 1 (at the sentinel φ, never achieved by any prime).

Inverting s to Recover p

Given any jump size s, we can recover exactly which prime produced it by solving the quadratic sp² − sp − 1 = 0:

Prime Recovery Formula
p  =  (1 + √(1 + 4/s)) / 2 At s = 1:   φ = (1 + √5) / 2 ≈ 1.6180339887…   — the Golden Ratio Sentinel φ is the unique fixed point of the formula at s = 1. No prime produces s = 1, so φ is permanently unreachable — it is the infimum of the range of p(s) over all actual primes.

The golden ratio emerges not from geometry here but from the algebraic structure of prime jumps: it is the value the recovery formula converges toward as jump sizes approach their maximum. Every prime p satisfies p > φ or p = 2 (the only prime below φ), and the sequence of recovered values p(s_p) crowds toward φ from above as primes grow.

Section 2 · The Prime Zeta Function

How C(x) Encodes the Prime Zeta Function

The Prime Zeta Function is defined as the Dirichlet series summing p−s over all primes:

Prime Zeta Function
P(s)  =  ∑p prime p−s  =  2−s + 3−s + 5−s + 7−s + … Related to the Riemann zeta function via: P(s) = ∑_{n≥1} μ(n)/n · log ζ(ns), where μ is the Möbius function.

The log C(x) as a Prime Zeta Weight

Taking the logarithm of C(x) converts the product into a sum over primes — directly analogous to the prime zeta function at s = 2:

Logarithm of C(x)
log C(x)  =  ∑p ≤ x log ((p² − 2)/(p² − 1))          ≈  −∑p ≤ x 1/(p² − 1)    [to leading order in 1/p²]          ≈  −P(2) + O(x−2)   as x → ∞ The infinite product C∞ = ∏_p (p²−2)/(p²−1) ≈ 0.5307… is directly controlled by P(2) = ∑ p⁻² ≈ 0.4522…

The Inverse 1/C(x) Mimics π(x)

The inverse function 1/C(x) = ∏p≤x (p²−1)/(p²−2) grows step by step at each prime, tracking the prime counting function π(x). This is not coincidental:

Logarithmic Derivative Connection
d/dx log(1/C(x))  =  −d/dx log C(x)  =  ∑p s_p · δ(x − p) Each prime p contributes a Dirac spike of weight s_p = 1/(p(p−1)) — exactly the jump size. This is the Lift Survival analog of the von Mangoldt function Λ(n), which encodes primes in the logarithmic derivative of the Riemann zeta function.

The Prime Zeta at s = 2

The sum ∑p p−2 = P(2) ≈ 0.45224742… controls the infinite product C∞. More precisely:

Exact Relation at Infinity
log C∞  =  ∑p log((p²−2)/(p²−1))          =  −∑p log(1 + 1/(p²−2))          ≈  −P(2)   +   O(P(4))   (higher-order prime zeta corrections) C∞ ≈ 0.5307… exactly, with corrections from P(4) = ∑ p⁻⁴ ≈ 0.00772…, P(6) ≈ 0.000264…, etc. The full expansion is: log C∞ = −∑_{k≥1} P(2k)/(k·2^k) approximately.

This means C∞ is not arbitrary — it is a precise analytic invariant of the primes, expressible as an Euler product evaluated at the prime zeta values. Every digit of 0.5307… encodes information about the distribution of primes.
