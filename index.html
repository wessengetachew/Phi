The Quadratic Family
Fibonacci to Primes

Every prime number sits on the same quadratic curve that generates the Fibonacci sequence. The parameter s — called the jump size — measures exactly how far each prime is from the golden ratio φ. At s = 1, the curve produces φ exactly. At every other s, it produces a prime.

This is not a coincidence. It is a consequence of a single algebraic identity connecting modular arithmetic, prime distribution, and the Fibonacci recurrence.

§ 1 The Core Identity

One equation — two worlds

The quadratic sp² − sp − 1 = 0 is a one-parameter family indexed by s. Every value of s gives a different parabola. Every parabola has a positive root p₊(s) — a specific number where the curve crosses the x-axis from below.

At s = 1 → Fibonacci
1·p² − 1·p − 1 = 0
p² − p − 1 = 0
p₊ = (1 + √5) / 2 = φ

This is the characteristic equation of the
Fibonacci recurrence xₙ = xₙ₋₁ + xₙ₋₂
At s = 1/p(p−1) → prime p
s·p² − s·p − 1 = 0
s = 1/p(p−1)
p₊ = (1 + √(1 + 4/s)) / 2

This gives back p exactly.
Every prime has its own unique s.

The two sides share the same algebraic skeleton. φ is where the family begins. As s decreases from 1 toward 0, the positive root p₊ grows from φ through 2, 3, 5, 7, 11… — the prime sequence in exact order.

s = 1  →  p₊ = φ = 1.6180339887…   ← Fibonacci, the sentinel
s = 1/2  →  p₊ = 2   ← first prime, largest jump
s = 1/6  →  p₊ = 3
s = 1/20  →  p₊ = 5
s = 1/42  →  p₊ = 7
s = 1/p(p−1)  →  p₊ = p   ← exact recovery for every prime
s → 0  →  p₊ → ∞   ← large primes, tiny jumps
Key insight: φ is the permanent unreachable boundary. No prime produces s = 1. Every prime produces some s < 1. The distance φ − p₊ measures how far that prime is from the Fibonacci fixed point — and it is always positive, always shrinking as primes grow.
§ 2 What is the jump size s?

s is the fingerprint of each prime

The jump size s = 1/p(p−1) comes from the Lift Survival Framework. When a prime p enters the Euler product C(x) = ∏(p²−2)/(p²−1), the product drops by a multiplicative factor. The relative size of that drop — the fraction of coprime residues that the prime eliminates — is exactly s.

C(x) = ∏p ≤ x (p² − 2) / (p² − 1)   ← Lift Survival Function
At prime p:   ΔC / C = −s = −1/p(p−1)   ← the jump
Equivalently:   s = 1/p(p−1) = 1/2, 1/6, 1/20, 1/42, …

So s encodes a real arithmetic event — the drop in coprime density when prime p enters the sieve. The quadratic sp² − sp − 1 = 0 inverts this: given the drop s, it recovers which prime caused it. This is exact for every prime, with no approximation.

The Parabola — f(p) = sp² − sp − 1
The main curve. It changes shape as you move s. At s = 1 it is steep and gold (Fibonacci). As s decreases it flattens and shifts teal (prime territory). Where it crosses the x-axis is p₊ — the recovered prime or φ.

φ Vertical Line
The golden ratio φ ≈ 1.618. A permanent vertical dashed line. The parabola always has its positive root to the right of this line for primes, and exactly on it at s = 1. It is the sentinel: no prime can push the root leftward past it.

p₊ Root Marker (Gold rings)
The positive root of the current quadratic. Three concentric rings centered where the parabola crosses the x-axis. Gold when s is arbitrary. Green when s exactly matches a prime's jump size. This is the number recovered by the formula.

p₋ Root Marker (Coral dot)
The negative root. Always sits left of zero on the x-axis, below zero in value. Algebraically real but arithmetically irrelevant — no prime is negative. It completes the quadratic but plays no role in prime recovery.

Prime Dots (Teal circles)
Fixed teal dots on the x-axis at every prime p in view. They do not move. They mark the positions where the positive root lands when s = 1/p(p−1). Their height above the axis (tick mark) is proportional to the jump size — taller for smaller primes.

When the animation runs, or as you drag the slider, the path that p₊ traces along the x-axis is recorded in violet. It shows the continuous journey from φ at s = 1 through every prime as s decreases. Enable with the Trace layer toggle.

Tangent Line (Teal dashes)
The slope of the parabola at the positive root p₊. The tangent is always positive here (the curve is rising). Its steepness tells you how sensitive p₊ is to small changes in s — steeper tangent means the root moves faster with s.

Fibonacci Spiral + Rectangles
The golden spiral and golden rectangle series drawn at the φ position. Visible when s is near 1. The spiral is a logarithmic spiral with growth ratio φ. The rectangles show the φ:1 ratio decomposing into itself. Enable/disable with the Fibonacci layer toggle.

Vertex Marker (White dot)
The minimum of the parabola, which always sits at p = 1/2 regardless of s. The vertex y-value is −s/4 − 1, which approaches −1 as s → 0 (flat line). Helps you see how the parabola changes shape.
◌
Filled area between roots
The shaded region between p₋ and p₊ where f(p) < 0 — where the parabola dips below the x-axis. This region contains the interval [p₋, p₊]. Its width grows as s decreases and p₊ moves right toward larger primes.
§ 5 Controls — How to Use the Explorer

Every control explained

s (linear)
Move s from 0 to 1 linearly. Start at 1 (φ) and drag left to watch the parabola sweep through each prime. The number box accepts any decimal up to 10 places.
s (log scale)
Navigate to very small s values for large primes — 10⁻⁹ lets you reach primes in the billions. The linear slider compresses everything near zero so use log scale for p > 30.
Jump to prime
Click any prime button (p=2 through p=997) to set s exactly to 1/p(p−1). The root snaps to that prime precisely. The dot turns green. The graph centers on that prime.
Parabola
Toggle the main curve. Even off, the root markers and prime dots remain. Useful when studying just the root positions without the visual mass of the parabola.
Roots
Toggle the p₊ and p₋ root markers. The concentric-ring gold marker and the small coral dot below the axis.
Primes
Toggle the fixed teal prime dots. When off, you see only the moving root with no reference points. When on, every prime in view is marked with its label and jump size.
Fibonacci
Toggle the golden spiral and rectangle overlay at φ. Most visible when s is near 1. Turn off for a cleaner view when working with small primes.
p₊ trace
Toggle the violet trail that records where p₊ has been. Cleared when toggled off and back on. Most useful during animation — you see the full path from φ through every prime.
Tangent
Toggle the teal dashed tangent line at the root. Shows the local slope f′(p₊) = 2s·p₊ − s. The steeper this is, the more the root position depends on s.
Animate
Sweeps s continuously from 1 down to near 0 and back. Watch the parabola travel from the Fibonacci equation through every prime. The speed slider controls pace.
Hover canvas
Move your mouse over the canvas to see f(p) at any point — the equation value, distance to φ, and nearest prime. The tooltip tracks your position in real time.
Click prime dot
Click any teal prime dot to lock s to that prime's exact jump size. The dot turns green. Click again to unlock. The status bar shows all computed values for that prime.

§ 7 Why Does This Work?

The algebra in plain language

Start with any prime p. Define s = 1/p(p−1). Then plug s into the quadratic sp² − sp − 1 = 0 and check whether p satisfies it:

s·p² − s·p − 1
= [1/p(p−1)]·p² − [1/p(p−1)]·p − 1
= p²/p(p−1) − p/p(p−1) − 1
= p/(p−1) − 1/(p−1) − 1
= (p−1)/(p−1) − 1
= 1 − 1 = 0 ✓

So p is always a root of the quadratic formed by its own jump size. The recovery formula just inverts this: given s, solve for p. The positive root p₊ = (1 + √(1 + 4/s))/2 is that inversion — the quadratic formula applied to sp² − sp − 1 = 0.

At s = 1 the formula gives p = (1 + √5)/2 = φ. The connection to Fibonacci is not approximate — it is exact, because the Fibonacci characteristic equation x² − x − 1 = 0 is precisely this quadratic evaluated at s = 1.

The sentinel property: φ > 2 > 3? No — φ ≈ 1.618 < 2. Every prime is greater than φ. So the positive root p₊ is always above φ for primes. φ is the infimum of the set {p₊(s) : p is prime} and is never achieved. That is why it is called the Golden Ratio Sentinel.
